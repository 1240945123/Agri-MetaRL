ABSTRACT

Greenhouse climate control is an important problem in smart agriculture. The problem has two goals: first, to ensure suitable growing conditions for crops; second, to reduce energy consumption and improve economic returns. Greenhouse systems have three characteristics: strong nonlinearity, time-varying external disturbances, and changing crop growth stages. These characteristics make traditional methods prone to failure. Traditional methods include PID, rule-based control, and MPC. MPC relies heavily on accurate models. Under cross-season and cross-weather conditions, the control performance of these methods tends to degrade. Reinforcement learning (RL) offers a data-driven path for greenhouse control. But most existing studies train a single policy in a single environment. These studies pay insufficient attention to cross-task transfer and fast adaptation.

To address the above issues, this paper formulates greenhouse control as a set of related tasks. These tasks are defined jointly by weather year, start date, and scenario conditions. This paper proposes a new meta-reinforcement learning algorithm, Agri-MetaRL. The algorithm does not simply apply off-the-shelf RL to greenhouse control. Instead, it improves the advantage estimation step at the algorithmic level. Based on Recurrent PPO, this paper introduces a support-query style advantage correction mechanism during training before policy updates. Specifically, this paper designs and implements a custom module, MetaAdvantageHead. This module encodes task context from support trajectories and performs task-adaptive correction of generalized advantage estimation on query trajectories during optimization. This paper does not introduce extra policy branches or heavy bi-level optimizers. Under this premise, the learned update mechanism is intended to improve the policy's robustness under task distribution changes.

Experiments were conducted on the GreenLight-Gym greenhouse benchmark environment. Under 5 random seeds, Agri-MetaRL achieved a final mean training return of 3955. This value is higher than Recurrent PPO's 3886 and PPO's 3811. On the held-out weather distribution, for Agri-MetaRL, the mean return per seed for training and test tasks was 3955 ± 16 and 4022 ± 83, respectively. The method maintained competitive held-out performance under the present protocol. Under the fixed protocol, Agri-MetaRL had the lowest violation steps for temperature, humidity, and CO2. Agri-MetaRL achieved the highest EPI of 3.46 €/m². This value is higher than Recurrent PPO's 3.34 €/m² and PPO's 3.29 €/m².


INTRODUCTION

Protected agriculture provides stable growing conditions for crops by regulating temperature, humidity, CO2 concentration, and radiation. This approach can increase yield per unit area, extend supply periods, and resist extreme weather. In modern high-tech greenhouses, environmental variables, crop state, and actuators are strongly coupled. External weather and crop growth stages change continuously. Thus, greenhouse control has characteristics of strong time-variation, partial observability, and multi-objective optimization. How to achieve high returns, low energy consumption, and strong generalization control while ensuring crop safety bounds is one of the core problems at the intersection of agricultural engineering and intelligent control.

Traditional greenhouse control often uses PID, threshold rules, and manual experience-based strategies. These methods are simple to implement and highly interpretable. But parameters are usually tuned for specific seasons, specific greenhouses, and specific crop stages. When weather distribution changes or crop state shifts, re-tuning is often required. Model predictive control (MPC) can use future predictions and explicit constraints to improve control quality. But MPC performance depends on high-precision greenhouse dynamics models and stable solving processes. When model error, parameter uncertainty, and long-horizon rolling optimization coexist, deployment cost remains high. This has been reflected in studies on economic greenhouse MPC and full-cycle crop setpoint optimization. RL-guided MPC and RL-based MPC have been proposed as compromise solutions to balance learning ability and optimization structure.

In recent years, reinforcement learning (RL) has received wide attention. RL does not rely on precise analytical models and can directly optimize policies toward cumulative returns. GreenLight-Gym provides a standardized high-fidelity benchmark environment for greenhouse RL. Greenhouse RL and deep RL control have been used in recent years for comparing RL with MPC, improving energy efficiency, and introducing interactive control. But most existing work limits training to a single or nearly single environment distribution. As a result, the learned policies are often closer to "a strong controller trained on one representative task" than to "a task-aware update structure that transfers across a family of related tasks."

From a learning perspective, changes in weather year, start planting date, parameter perturbations, and scenario coefficients make greenhouse control better viewed as a family of related tasks rather than a single MDP. The goal of meta-reinforcement learning (Meta-RL) is not only to learn a static optimal policy, but also to learn an update structure that transfers across related tasks. Meta-gradient RL, distributional meta-gradient RL, first-order meta-learning, and comparisons of fine-tuning with Meta-RL all show that learning update rules or adaptation priors can significantly affect cross-task performance. Inspired by this line of work, this paper proposes a new meta-reinforcement learning algorithm, Agri-MetaRL, and formulates greenhouse control as a Meta-RL problem under a weather-driven task distribution. The contribution of this paper lies in algorithm design, not merely in applying off-the-shelf RL to greenhouse scenarios. Instead of adding multi-policy branches or constructing heavy inner-outer optimizers, this paper performs task-adaptive correction on advantage estimation. Specifically, task context is extracted from support trajectories, and corrected advantages are generated on query trajectories to guide PPO policy updates. This design aligns with the research thread of improving RL algorithms by learning optimization objectives or update rules.

Unlike the traditional "single-environment RL + test-period extrapolation," this paper introduces a task-partitioned training skeleton at the implementation level. Each task segment in the training buffer is split into support and query sets. The support set encodes context. The query set computes task-adaptive advantages for the auxiliary correction module. This design brings Agri-MetaRL closer to task-conditioned policy optimization while retaining training-flow compatibility with standard PPO and engineering simplicity.

The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
(1) For greenhouse climate control, a weather-driven task distribution modeling approach is proposed, and training and testing protocols for agricultural meta-reinforcement learning are given.
(2) A new meta-RL algorithm design is proposed: task-adaptive correction is introduced at the advantage estimation step, rather than merely applying off-the-shelf RL to greenhouse scenarios.
(3) The custom module MetaAdvantageHead is designed and implemented. It performs lightweight task-conditioned advantage correction through support-set context encoding. This module differs from standard Recurrent PPO and is a researcher-developed algorithm component.
(4) On the GreenLight-Gym benchmark, a unified evaluation matrix is constructed including learning curves, climate bounds, economic metrics, and held-out evaluation.
(5) An implementation skeleton and figure/table asset generation pipeline for paper reproduction are completed, laying the foundation for multi-seed extension experiments and the English submission version.


MATERIALS AND METHODS

Simulation Environment

Experiments in this paper were conducted on the GreenLight-Gym greenhouse reinforcement learning platform. The platform exposes high-fidelity greenhouse climate dynamics, crop growth, and actuator control through the Gymnasium interface. It supports flexible configuration of weather, parameters, and observation modules. The simulation object is a Venlo-type glass greenhouse common in the Netherlands, with tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) as the crop. External weather uses Amsterdam historical weather data. Each episode represents a 60 d growth window. The control step is 15 min. Thus each episode contains 5760 control steps.

Observation and Action Spaces

The agent's observation vector has 263 dimensions. It consists of six types of information: indoor climate (4), crop state (3), control signals (6), current weather (5), time encoding (5), and weather forecast (240). Observations include indoor climate, crop state, current control inputs, current weather, time encoding, and weather forecasts for the next 48 time steps. Running mean and variance were used to normalize observations during training. The action space is a 6-dimensional continuous vector. It corresponds to six actuator types: heating, CO2 injection, thermal screen, ventilation, supplemental lighting, and shade screen. The action range is [-1, 1]. The system updates control inputs in an incremental manner, where the maximum increment is 0.1. The incremental control mechanism can reduce abrupt actuator changes and improve control smoothness and training stability.

Reward Function and Constraints

This paper adopts the profit-oriented reward function in GreenLight-Gym. The single-step reward is defined as fruit mass increment times tomato price minus heating, CO2, and electricity costs minus penalty-weighted constraint violation. The safe ranges for temperature, relative humidity, and CO2 are: Temperature 15–34 °C, Relative humidity 50–85%, CO2 concentration 300–1600 ppm. The penalty weights are given. This approach of incorporating safety bounds into policy optimization through penalty terms is consistent with classical practice in constrained reinforcement learning.

Task Distribution and Meta-RL Formulation

We view greenhouse control as a meta-reinforcement learning problem. We define a single control task as weather trajectory, start date, greenhouse parameters, and scenario parameters. Weather year and start date cause task differences. So we use 2010 weather as the training distribution. We randomly select start dates between day 59 and day 96. We use 2011–2013 weather as the test distribution. We test on days 59, 80, and 100.

For each task segment, we define the support set and query set. The support set encodes task context for the auxiliary correction module. The query set provides the trajectories on which corrected advantages are computed and used for optimization. The objective of Agri-MetaRL maximizes expected return over the task distribution. Here, the objective denotes the support-conditioned advantage correction mechanism used during training, rather than a separate inner-loop policy optimization step. The system includes a task distribution affected by weather, the greenhouse environment, and the Agri-MetaRL agent. Different years provide different weather conditions. The agent includes the support set, recurrent Actor-Critic networks, and the meta-adaptation module. The agent receives greenhouse state and observations. The agent outputs actions to the greenhouse. The greenhouse returns rewards to the agent.

Base Policy: Recurrent PPO

Agri-MetaRL uses Recurrent PPO as the base learner. PPO constrains policy update magnitude through a clipped objective. GAE provides a low-variance, stable approximation for advantage estimation. Recurrent PPO further introduces LSTM to handle partial observability and temporal dependencies. This paper adopts a separated actor-critic LSTM structure. Both the policy and value networks use 1-layer, 256 hidden-unit LSTMs. The policy head fully connected layers are [1024, 1024]. The value head is [128, 128]. The learning rate is 1.161×10⁻⁴, gamma=0.9631, GAE lambda=0.9666, batch size is 512, and training epochs are 8. These hyperparameters mainly follow the GreenLight-Gym and sb3-contrib Recurrent PPO default configurations and common settings in greenhouse RL work. They were selected after preliminary grid search to ensure comparability with baseline methods.

Agri-MetaRL: Support-Query Advantage Correction

Unlike typical Meta-RL methods, MAML learns a quickly adaptable initialization through inner-outer optimization loops. PEARL conditions the policy on latent context variables. Both require extra gradient computation or context inference modules, and policy structure can vary with task. This paper adopts a different design. On the basis of Recurrent PPO, only the advantage estimation step is corrected in a task-aware manner during training. The policy network remains single and does not switch with task. Meta-learning is embodied in how the advantage signal used for policy gradients is adjusted according to support-derived task context. This design does not require MAML-style inner update loops. It also does not inject context into policy input like PEARL. Instead, support-query partitioning conditions the auxiliary advantage correction module. In this way, the method aims to improve cross-task generalization while maintaining training-flow compatibility with standard PPO and engineering simplicity. The unique contribution of this paper is the introduction of a support-query structure into the advantage estimation step of RL. Through the lightweight MetaAdvantageHead, the method learns how to correct advantages rather than adding task-specific policy branches.

Unlike traditional PPO and Recurrent PPO, which use the same GAE-style advantages for policy updates on all data, Agri-MetaRL splits each task segment into support and query sets. The support set encodes task context such as weather and start date. On the query set, task-relevant correction is applied to the raw advantages. The corrected advantage is then used for policy updates. In this way, the training signal can vary with task context. PPO and Recurrent PPO tend to learn fixed policies on single or nearly single environment distributions. Performance may therefore degrade when weather years or start dates change. Agri-MetaRL instead models greenhouse control as a task distribution and learns a support-conditioned advantage correction rule during training. This design is intended to improve cross-task robustness without modifying the PPO backbone, adding extra policy branches, or introducing heavy inner-outer optimizers.

MetaAdvantageHead is the above lightweight task-context-guided mechanism. It performs task-adaptive correction of advantage estimation before policy updates. It contains three modules. First, each collected task segment is partitioned into support and query parts. The support set ratio is 50%. The minimum support steps are 256. Conceptually, this corresponds to an early support portion and a later query portion within the segment. Then, the support set enters the task context encoder. The encoder extracts statistics (e.g., mean and variance) from support set states, advantages, rewards, and returns. It outputs a compact context vector to characterize the current task background. Meanwhile, the query set is processed by the base Recurrent PPO module to obtain the current observation and raw advantage. Next, the advantage correction head receives three inputs: context vector, current observation, and raw advantage. It outputs a correction term through the learnable network. The correction term is added to the raw advantage to obtain the corrected advantage.

MetaAdvantageHead is a custom module in this paper. It is not a standard component in off-the-shelf RL libraries. Its architecture is as follows. The context encoder input dimension is 66. The encoder is a 2-layer fully connected network. Each layer is followed by LayerNorm and SiLU activation. The output context dimension is 64. The advantage correction head input consists of observation projection (64 dim), raw advantage (1 dim), and context (64 dim), totaling 129 dim. The correction head is a 2-layer MLP. The middle layer uses SiLU activation. The output is a scalar correction term. The corrected advantage is sent to the policy update module for PPO policy gradients. It also participates in meta-loss computation after clipping and standardization to drive learning. The meta-loss combines squared error between corrected advantage and return-based target, plus a regularization term. Beta is set to 0.05. This design preserves the PPO base structure. By making the corrected advantage approach the return-based target, the auxiliary module is encouraged to learn task-sensitive corrections that are useful for training.

From a mechanistic perspective, the chain support set to task context to advantage correction to improved generalization can be summarized as follows. (1) Support set encodes task context: support trajectories implicitly contain information such as weather year, start day, diurnal rhythm, and crop stage. The context encoder extracts summary statistics from support set states, advantages, rewards, and returns. It outputs compact context to characterize the current task segment. (2) Advantage correction adjusts with task: after context is input to the correction head, the correction term varies with task context. Therefore, on the query set, the scale and direction of advantages at each time step can be adjusted. Under different tasks, the same observation and raw advantage may correspond to different correction amounts. (3) Corrected advantage guides policy gradient: the corrected advantage participates in PPO policy gradient computation. Thus, policy updates are shaped by both the base GAE signal and the support-conditioned correction. The meta-loss encourages the correction head to learn corrections that move the corrected advantage toward the return-based target. (4) Cross-task robustness: if the context encoder captures features that are stable across related greenhouse tasks, the learned correction rule can provide a more task-aware training signal. This, in turn, can contribute to better performance on held-out weather distributions without introducing task-specific policy branches.

Training and Testing Protocol

Total training steps are 2×10⁶. The number of parallel environments is 8. Model selection uses deterministic evaluation. Approximately every 2×10⁵ steps, evaluation is run once on the evaluation environment (training distribution: year 2010, day 59). The checkpoint with the highest mean reward is saved. This paper uses a fixed protocol for evaluation. PPO, Recurrent PPO, Agri-MetaRL, and the rule-based baseline are compared on single-episode performance in a deterministic environment. Multi-seed testing is conducted on both training weather distribution and held-out weather distribution.

Experimental Design and Statistical Analysis

To evaluate method robustness, each algorithm was trained and evaluated with 5 independent random seeds. Seeds were fixed. During training, each episode's start date was randomly sampled within the task definition range. Weather year was partitioned by training/test distribution. During testing, deterministic environment and fixed start days were used to eliminate evaluation-stage randomness. Performance comparison between methods is reported as mean ± standard deviation over 5 replicates. Given the small sample size, this paper mainly performs descriptive statistics. For inferential tests, paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test can be used to compare return differences between methods under the same seed.

Evaluation Metrics

This paper mainly uses the following metrics for evaluation: episode total return, temperature/humidity/CO2 violation steps, cumulative economic metrics (Revenue, Heat, Electricity, CO2, EPI), and train-vs-test distribution comparison. To analyze control behavior, this paper also presents indoor climate evolution and key actuator trajectories over the full 60 d cycle.

Software and Reproducibility

Experiments used Python 3.11, Stable-Baselines3, sb3-contrib, PyTorch, and GreenLight-Gym. Unlike studies that only use off-the-shelf software and hardware, this paper implements a custom MetaAdvantageHead module on top of Recurrent PPO. This module is a researcher-developed algorithm component. It is not included in the standard implementation of Stable-Baselines3 or sb3-contrib. To support paper reproduction, this paper further supplements a unified figure and table asset generation script. So figures and data summaries in the main manuscript can be generated directly from the workspace.


RESULTS

Learning Curves and Final Returns

The mean training learning curves of PPO, Recurrent PPO, and Agri-MetaRL under 5 random seeds show that Agri-MetaRL maintains a lead from the early-mid training phase. Recurrent PPO is consistently higher than PPO overall. Agri-MetaRL reaches 3955 ± 16. This is higher than Recurrent PPO's 3886 ± 32 and PPO's 3811 ± 8. These results suggest that support-query advantage correction improves final performance and is associated with stable behavior across random seeds.

Climate Constraint Performance

Agri-MetaRL performs best on all three dimensions: temperature, humidity, and CO2. Violation steps are 97 ± 44, 98 ± 29, and 11 ± 5, respectively. Recurrent PPO is second with 110 ± 55, 166 ± 33, and 28 ± 8. PPO has 118 ± 28, 172 ± 68, and 33 ± 12. Overall, Agri-MetaRL has a clear advantage in climate constraint control.

Economic Metrics Comparison

Compared with the rule-based baseline, all three RL methods significantly reduce electricity and CO2 costs and improve overall economic returns. Under multi-seed statistics, Agri-MetaRL has Revenue of 11.56 ± 0.12 €/m², Heat of 8.12 ± 0.32 €/m², CO2 cost of 0.08 ± 0.02 €/m², and final EPI of 3.46 ± 0.30 €/m². This is higher than Recurrent PPO's 3.34 ± 0.22 €/m² and PPO's 3.29 ± 0.14 €/m². These results suggest that support-query advantage correction can improve the balance between returns and resource consumption. The rule-based baseline has higher Revenue, but its Electricity and CO2 costs are much larger. Therefore, its overall EPI remains significantly negative (-4.76 €/m²).

Return denotes episode total return. EPI is the Economic Production Index. TWB are the time-within-bounds percentages for CO2, temperature, and relative humidity, respectively, converted from violation steps over the 5760-step episode. The training distribution used year 2010 weather with fixed start day (day 59). The test distribution used 2011–2013 held-out weather with start days randomly sampled from 59/80/100.

EPI and Return on the test distribution are both higher than on the training distribution. A likely reason is that the test set includes later start days such as day 80 and 100. These runs correspond to the mid-to-late growth season (approximately late March to early June), when day length is longer, heating demand is lower, and photosynthetic potential is higher. Amsterdam weather in 2011–2013 may also provide more favorable radiation and temperature conditions than 2010 in some periods. Therefore, the higher Test EPI should be interpreted mainly as a seasonal evaluation effect rather than a direct indicator of controller superiority.

60 d Full-Cycle Control Behavior Analysis

To further observe the long-term regulation characteristics of the controller over the full growth window, the actual simulation trajectories of Agri-MetaRL over the full 60 d cycle under the fixed protocol (year 2010, day 59) include indoor temperature, relative humidity, CO2 concentration, and temporal changes of heating, ventilation, and CO2 injection. The climate subplots show upper and lower safety bounds.

Agri-MetaRL shows relatively stable diurnal rhythm and clear phased regulation logic over long time scales. The temperature trajectory stays within the safe range most of the time. Only short dips occur in a few cold-start phases at night. This is consistent with its temperature violation steps of 97 ± 44. CO2 maintains high levels during the day and naturally decreases at night. A small number of violations exist on some seeds (11 ± 5 steps). Overall control quality is acceptable. From the control inputs, Agri-MetaRL's heating, ventilation, and CO2 injection all show long-term coupling consistent with climate state. Heating is increased in low-temperature or low-radiation phases. Ventilation is enhanced in high-temperature and high-humidity periods. CO2 injection is mainly during daytime when photosynthesis is active. Overall, this long-horizon simulation figure, together with training learning curves, climate violation statistics, and economic metrics, shows that Agri-MetaRL's advantages are not only reflected in instantaneous response in short time windows. They are more reflected in the comprehensive balance of climate stability, control smoothness, and return-oriented behavior over the full 60 d control process.


DISCUSSION

The results of this paper show that explicitly modeling greenhouse control as a task distribution and introducing support-query advantage correction at the PPO update level is a meaningful direction to investigate. Agri-MetaRL outperforms PPO in final training return. It also achieves higher Return and EPI on the held-out weather distribution than on the training distribution. This pattern is consistent with the view that task-context-based updates may improve robustness under distribution changes, although the train-test gap should be interpreted together with the seasonal differences in the evaluation protocol.

Compared with existing greenhouse RL studies, Morcego et al. compared DDPG with MPC on the GreenLight model and demonstrated RL potential in greenhouse climate control. Ajagekar et al. combined deep RL with robust optimization for energy-efficient control of semi-closed greenhouses. Xiao et al. introduced a human-machine collaborative RL framework with grower participation. These studies mostly focus on single-environment or single-policy optimization. In contrast, this paper explicitly treats weather year and start day as task variables and introduces support-query updates during training. Under the present benchmark protocol, the resulting policy maintains competitive performance on held-out weather distributions. GreenLight-Gym provides a standardized benchmark for greenhouse RL, and this paper further constructs a unified protocol including held-out evaluation. This facilitates comparison with subsequent studies.

From the perspective of control objective decomposition, Agri-MetaRL's advantages are mainly reflected in two aspects: final training return and economic returns (EPI). Agri-MetaRL outperforms Recurrent PPO and PPO on EPI, Revenue, and climate constraints. This suggests that task-adaptive updates help the controller form a better balance between returns and costs. Agri-MetaRL has the lowest violation steps on all three climate dimensions: temperature, humidity, and CO2. This is consistent with the comprehensive ranking in the learning curves. While maintaining compatibility with standard PPO and not introducing heavy inner-outer optimizers, return improved from PPO's 3811 to Agri-MetaRL's 3955 (about 3.8%). EPI improved from 3.29 to 3.46 €/m² (about 5.2%). Although this improvement is moderate, it still has two implications. First, the lightweight design facilitates integration into existing greenhouse control systems because no extra policy branches or complex optimization procedures are needed. Second, the held-out results suggest that the advantage correction mechanism can improve cross-task robustness rather than only fitting the training distribution.

At the same time, this paper also observes clear limitations. The results are still mainly based on the current benchmark and task partitioning. Further validation of robustness is needed under richer parameter perturbations, longer time spans, and larger-scale multi-seed experiments. Future work can continue to optimize reward design, context feature construction, and multi-objective trade-offs. From a practical application perspective, Agri-MetaRL's lightweight design (no added policy branches, no heavy inner-outer optimizers) is conducive to integration into existing greenhouse control systems. The trade-off between support set length and computational cost still needs attention. In addition, simulation-to-reality transfer requires further validation in real greenhouses, especially for sensor noise, model bias, and delay.

This paper should also be viewed as an engineering prototype for Meta-RL-oriented greenhouse control, not as the final answer to the greenhouse meta-learning problem. The current implementation already has a clear support/query meta-update skeleton and unified figure generation pipeline. But to form stronger statistical evidence, extension experiments are still needed with more seeds, richer task distributions, and more complete multi-baseline comparisons.


CONCLUSION

This paper proposes Agri-MetaRL, an agricultural meta-reinforcement learning algorithm for greenhouse climate control. The method formulates greenhouse control tasks as a task distribution driven by weather year and start day. On the basis of Recurrent PPO, it introduces the support-query MetaAdvantageHead. Support-derived task context is used to correct query advantages during training. Compared with traditional single-environment RL, Agri-MetaRL places more emphasis on a cross-task transferable update structure.

Under the current experimental setup, Agri-MetaRL achieved higher final training returns. It also maintained relatively stable performance on the held-out weather distribution. Climate constraint analysis and economic metrics comparison further support the effectiveness of task-context encoding and advantage correction. Overall, the results indicate that Agri-MetaRL provides a practical and extensible framework for Meta-RL-oriented greenhouse climate control research.
